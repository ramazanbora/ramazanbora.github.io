Thesis Title
======
**Granularity in time, space, and behavior: Essays in empirical economics using open data**

Abstract
======
This thesis uses granular open-source data to study empirical questions across three dimensions: space, time, and behavior. The central argument is that coarse aggregation can hide meaningful institutional and behavioral patterns. Each chapter builds a pipeline from fragmented raw sources into harmonized econometric datasets, then applies identification-focused statistical designs to test chapter-specific hypotheses.

Chapter Summaries
======

1. **Granularity in Space: Centralization and Rural Development in Turkey**  
Using Turkey's 2012 metropolitan reform, this chapter combines reconstructed village boundary micro-polygons with VIIRS night lights, village populations, and driving-distance measures. Border-based DiD estimates show higher post-reform luminosity and stronger rural population retention in treated villages, with evidence of distance penalties and staged service rollout.

2. **Granularity in Time: Birthplace Favoritism Revisited**  
This chapter replicates classic birthplace favoritism findings in an open-source framework and extends them to the VIIRS era. Monthly-resolution evidence shows immediate favoritism at leadership transitions, persistence after departure, and a COVID-era premium partly attenuated by dense controls and leader-region comparisons.

3. **Granularity in Behavior: Ramadan Exposure and Chess Performance**  
From 16 million moves across 800,000 online games, this chapter studies Ramadan exposure with player and period fixed effects. Results show fewer mistakes, faster moves, and stronger nocturnal activity among exposed players, with gains concentrated among lower-skill and lower-experience groups.

Thesis Motivation
======
The thesis asks a common question across different settings: what becomes visible when measurement is made more granular? Across institutions, politics, and cognition, the evidence suggests that finer spatial, temporal, and behavioral data can overturn coarse-data conclusions and generate new policy-relevant insight.
