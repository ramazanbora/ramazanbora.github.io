Application for Research Fellow (Job No. 687647)
Dear Professor Smyth and Selection Committee,
I am writing to apply for the position of Research Fellow (Job No. 687647) at Monash University. I am currently a PhD candidate in Economics at the University of Queensland, with my thesis under examination and expected completion in early 2026. My research sits at the intersection of empirical economics, political economy, and computational social science, and centres on using granular open data to study institutions, governance, and decision-making through rigorous quantitative analysis. I am excited by the opportunity to contribute to the project An Empirical Study of Agenda Setting in the High Court of Australia, and believe my background is strongly aligned with the role’s emphasis on data-intensive, rigorous, collaborative empirical research.
My work has consistently involved transforming complex, fragmented, or under-structured data into usable research datasets and producing credible empirical analysis from them. Across my PhD and research appointments, I have developed expertise in large-scale data construction, panel data econometrics, causal inference, difference-in-differences, event studies, GIS-based analysis, and reproducible workflow design, using R, Stata, and Python. A central strength I would bring to this role is the ability to combine technical data skills with institutionally grounded research design.
My PhD thesis, Granularity in time, space, and behavior: Essays in empirical economics using open data, brings together a coherent research agenda centred on using granularity in space, time, and behaviour to uncover institutional and behavioural dynamics that are often obscured by coarse aggregation. In one project, I study local government centralisation and rural development in Turkey by reconstructing high-resolution administrative boundaries and linking them to remote sensing, population, and travel-distance data, then implementing a contiguous-border difference-in-differences design. In another, I revisit the political economy literature on leader birthplace favoritism using fully open-source data, monthly satellite night lights, and high-dimensional panel methods; this work is currently released as a UQ School of Economics Discussion Paper (2025). In a further project, I build a move-level panel from approximately 800,000 online chess games (around 16 million moves) and benchmark decision quality using the Stockfish engine, demonstrating my ability to manage and analyse large behavioural datasets at scale. Earlier in my training, my master’s research also involved formal economic modelling of organisational design and strategic communication, examining how centralised and decentralised decision-making perform under different information structures. Together, these projects show a consistent pattern: I identify questions that become tractable through granular open data, build original empirical pipelines from fragmented inputs, and apply careful identification and robustness strategies to produce credible answers.
I also have substantial experience in collaborative, externally engaged research environments. As a Senior Research Assistant at the Queensland Centre for Population Research, I helped develop a reproducible small-area population estimation pipeline for the Queensland Government Statistical Office, including methodological innovations in iterative proportional fitting and integerisation for Indigenous population estimation. In this role, I also liaised directly with QGSO stakeholders, which strengthened my ability to communicate technical work clearly in a policy-facing research setting. At the UQ International Comparisons Database, I refactored and documented the core R codebase, built data pipelines, and supported the delivery of public-facing dashboards. In another applied research role, I worked with large administrative property transaction data in collaboration with the NSW Valuer General, integrating these data with GIS and transport network information to construct measures for econometric analysis. These roles have strengthened my capacity to work both independently and as part of interdisciplinary teams, while maintaining high standards of organisation, documentation, stakeholder communication, and project delivery.
I am particularly drawn to this Monash position because it offers the opportunity to apply my empirical training to a new and intellectually exciting domain: agenda setting, institutions, and judicial behaviour in the High Court of Australia. Although my formal training is in economics, my research is fundamentally concerned with institutions, governance, decision-making, and the measurement of behaviour from complex real-world data. For that reason, I see a very strong fit between my background and the aims of this project. I would welcome the opportunity to contribute my quantitative expertise to empirical legal or socio-legal research, and I am enthusiastic about working in an interdisciplinary setting that brings together law, economics, and political analysis.
In addition to research, I bring a strong teaching and academic service background. I have taught across undergraduate, master’s, and PhD-level economics courses in Australia, the United States, and Turkey, including roles as Senior Tutor, Tutor, Teaching Assistant, and Instructor of Record. These experiences have strengthened my communication skills, my ability to explain complex ideas clearly, and my effectiveness in collaborative academic environments. I have also taken on coordination and administrative responsibilities in teaching and research settings, which I understand will be valuable for supporting research meetings, project administration, and team coordination in this role.
My academic pathway has not been linear, and I believe this has strengthened rather than diminished my suitability for a research-focused role. I have rebuilt my academic trajectory across countries, institutions, and research areas. That experience has developed resilience, adaptability, and a strong sense of purpose in my research career. I have re-established myself successfully in Australia, built a robust empirical research profile, and am now in the final stage of my PhD with a clear commitment to producing rigorous, policy-relevant scholarship.
I would be honoured to contribute to Monash University’s research community and to the ARC Discovery Project team. I am particularly attracted to Monash’s commitment to collaborative excellence, inclusion, and socially meaningful research, and I would welcome the opportunity to bring my quantitative research skills, data experience, and institution-focused research agenda to this position.
Thank you for your consideration. I have attached my CV and would be very pleased to discuss my application further.
Yours sincerely,
